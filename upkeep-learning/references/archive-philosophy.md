`learning/` is not a sidecar note set. It is a serious educational archive.

The job of this skill is to produce and maintain a repository-level body of teaching material that can support long-term mastery of:

- the project's current implementation,
- the project's stated mission and roadmap,
- the domain theory required to understand both,
- the major alternatives, comparisons, and trade-offs around the project.

## Core Standard

Optimise for pedagogical completeness.

For major concepts, systems, architectures, and domain areas, write enough that a motivated learner should not be left with obvious unresolved questions after reading.

This means the archive should be:

- exhaustive,
- verbose,
- highly explanatory,
- richly cross-linked,
- willing to repeat from a new angle when it improves understanding,
- comfortable with large file counts and long documents.

## What The Archive Is Not

It is not:

- a concise onboarding guide,
- a lightweight doc summary,
- a changelog,
- a set of thin wiki stubs,
- a “just enough to get by” knowledge base.

Those are failure modes for this skill.

## Scale Expectations

Large educational archives are expected.

It is normal for:

- a major concept file to be several hundred lines,
- a major system deep-dive to be several hundred lines,
- a central theory or architecture document to pass 1,000 lines when warranted,
- a domain area to span many connected files,
- multiple learning paths to overlap intentionally.

Do not compress content because the archive is becoming large. The archive is supposed to be large when the project warrants it.

## Teaching Standard

The archive should teach from multiple angles:

- intuition,
- formal definitions,
- mechanisms,
- worked examples,
- project grounding,
- limitations,
- misconceptions,
- alternatives,
- historical or roadmap context.

If a topic still feels under-explained after one pass, add more explanation.

## Tone Standard

Aim for graduate-level rigor with plain-language readability.

Write for an intelligent, motivated engineer who may not have formal academic training in the topic. The archive should be:

- rigorous without academic posturing,
- precise without jargon inflation,
- explanatory without sounding condescending,
- advanced without becoming opaque.

Do not write like a research paper unless the specific file genuinely benefits from that format. Teach clearly.

## Aggressive Instruction Standard

Do not be timid.

Do not write as if verbosity is dangerous.

Do not retreat into thin summaries because they feel safer.

Be explicit, elaborate, descriptive, comprehensive, and exhaustive.

## Failure Modes

Treat these as failures:

- leaving out major README-defined domains because they are not yet implemented,
- writing a short summary where a concept needs a real teaching document,
- omitting worked examples for difficult topics,
- omitting equations or notation where they would materially improve understanding,
- creating a beautiful navigation layer over shallow content,
- writing sparse glossary entries,
- stripping nuance out of comparisons and trade-offs,
- avoiding length because it feels too assertive.
